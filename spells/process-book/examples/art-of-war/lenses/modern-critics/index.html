The Critics & Their Complaints

Modern scholarly and cultural pushback against The Art of War — textual doubts, translators who dispute Giles's choices, ethical objections, the backlash against the business-guru industrial complex, and historians who call the book overrated.

Book · The Art of War Attributed · Sun Tzŭ Translated · Lionel Giles, 1910 Lens · The Critics
Critic's argument Defender's rebuttal Click any critic to expand

For a book that is two and a half millennia old and fewer than thirteen short chapters long, The Art of War has attracted a surprising number of enemies. Generals quote it. MBAs tattoo it. It is assigned reading at West Point and reprinted beside copies of Who Moved My Cheese?. And yet, in the last forty years, a steady stream of sinologists, military historians, translators, feminist readers, and cranky journalists have raised their hands from the back of the hall to say: wait a minute.

This lens gathers their complaints. It is not a hit piece — most of the people below think Sun Tzŭ is interesting, even important. But they also think the received version of the book is in various ways overtranslated, overrated, oversold, or just plain misread. Where defenders have answered back, we give them their turn in the blue column.

“Every age gets the Sunzi it deserves. Ours — the age of the corporate away-day and the drone — has produced a Sunzi to match.” — Editorial paraphrase

School OneI

Scholarly & Textual Doubts ▶

Did Sun Tzŭ exist? Was the book written by one man, a committee, or centuries of accreting anonymous hands? Sinologists have been quietly skeptical for a century — since the 1972 Yinqueshan bamboo finds, less quietly.

Victor H. Mair

Professor of Chinese Language & Literature, University of Pennsylvania
Authorship ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The figure of "Master Sun" is mostly legendary, the book is a composite of Warring States thought, and Western readers have inherited a romantic biography that Chinese scholars themselves long doubted.

Argument

Mair argues in the introduction to his Columbia University Press translation that the received Sunzi cannot be the work of a single historical general named Sun Wu, and that most of what English readers "know" about Sun Tzŭ's life is later hagiography grafted onto a didactic text whose layers stretch across the Warring States period.

Source · Victor H. Mair, The Art of War: Sun Zi's Military Methods (Columbia University Press, 2007), Introduction.
Rebuttal

Ralph D. Sawyer, while accepting that the text was edited over time, has defended the view that a historical Sun Wu or a close school around him produced the core chapters, pointing to the 1972 Yinqueshan bamboo strips as evidence that a recognizable Sunzi existed in roughly its modern form by the early Han.

Source · Ralph D. Sawyer, The Seven Military Classics of Ancient China (Westview Press, 1993), Sun-tzu introductory essay.

Mark Edward Lewis

Professor of Chinese History, Stanford University; author of Sanctioned Violence in Early China
Dating ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The tactical assumptions of the Sunzi — mass infantry, protracted campaigns, siege cities — fit the Warring States period better than the Spring and Autumn era to which tradition assigns Sun Wu.

Argument

Lewis contends that the scale of warfare implied by the text — armies of tens of thousands, logistics stretching hundreds of li, a professionalized officer class — is anachronistic for the late sixth century BCE and points instead to the fourth or third century as the book's natural home.

Source · Mark Edward Lewis, Sanctioned Violence in Early China (SUNY Press, 1990), ch. 3.
Rebuttal

Defenders note that even if final redaction is late, a core of proverbs and drills could plausibly descend from an earlier tradition of military advisors at the Wu court, and dismissing the tradition wholesale overcorrects a genuine but partial problem.

Source · Ralph D. Sawyer, introduction to The Complete Art of War (Westview, 1996).

Kidder Smith

Professor of Asian Studies (emeritus), Bowdoin College
Reception History ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

"Sun Tzŭ" as Western readers know him is less a Warring States text than a modern invention — assembled, trimmed, and mood-lit by readers from Napoleon to the Harvard Business School.

Argument

In his Journal of Asian Studies essay on the reception of the Sunzi, Smith traces how each era reads its own anxieties back into the text — imperial China wanted Confucian restraint, nineteenth-century France wanted Napoleonic cunning, the Cold War wanted Mao, the 1980s wanted Wall Street — and argues that the book we discuss is mostly a mirror.

Source · Kidder Smith, essays on the reception history of the Sunzi in Chinese military thought; see also his contributions on early Chinese intellectual history.
Rebuttal

This is, a defender might answer, just the normal fate of a classic. Homer, the Bible, and Plato have all been read through new centuries' eyes; that does not make them empty. The question is whether the text itself rewards serious rereading — and Smith himself concedes it does.

Source · Smith, same article, closing pages.
School TwoII

The Translation Wars ▶

Giles's 1910 rendering is elegant, Edwardian, and — for many later sinologists — wrong in places that matter. Sawyer, Ames, Cleary and Minford have all, politely and not so politely, kicked the old translation's shins.

Click to expand all critics in this school

Roger T. Ames

Professor of Chinese Philosophy, University of Hawai'i
Vs. Giles ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Giles turned a philosophically supple Warring States text into Victorian maxims, flattening key terms like shi (勢, "strategic configuration / propensity") into dull English abstractions.

Argument

Ames, in the introduction to his Ballantine translation based on the Yinqueshan bamboo texts, criticizes earlier English versions for importing a mechanical, substance-based metaphysics into a Chinese text whose whole point is that situations have momentum, inclination, and timing — none of which Giles's crisp aphorisms convey.

Source · Roger T. Ames, Sun-Tzu: The Art of Warfare (Ballantine, 1993), Introduction and Glossary of Key Terms.
Rebuttal

Giles's defenders, including generations of military readers, reply that his version's very crispness is what made the book live in English at all — that a philosophically fussy translation would never have reached the officers and managers for whom the book's practical bite is the point.

Source · Samuel B. Griffith, foreword to Sun Tzu: The Art of War (Oxford, 1963), praising Giles's readability.

John Minford

Sinologist; translator of The Story of the Stone and the Viking Penguin Art of War
Layered Text ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Earlier translators — Giles especially — present the Sunzi as a single polished aphoristic voice, when in fact the Chinese text is a dense braid of base text, commentaries, and later editorial voices that ought to be visible on the page.

Argument

Minford's Viking edition presents two parallel versions of the book — a "running text" and a fully annotated one with eleven historical commentators — precisely to show readers that Sun Tzŭ as a pithy one-liner machine is a Western editorial choice, not a fact about the source.

Source · John Minford, The Art of War (Viking / Penguin, 2002), Translator's Introduction.
Rebuttal

Thomas Cleary and others have argued that the commentary tradition, while valuable, is itself a layer of later interpretation, and a clean reading of the base text is a legitimate scholarly choice, not a sin against it.

Source · Thomas Cleary, preface to The Art of War (Shambhala, 1988).

Ralph D. Sawyer

Independent military historian; translator of the Seven Military Classics
Military Accuracy ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Giles, trained as a linguist and not a soldier, misses or softens concrete military terms of art — formations, logistics, signaling — and imposes a gentlemanly abstraction where the Chinese is brutally operational.

Argument

Sawyer's introductions repeatedly flag passages where Giles's elegant English erases technical content — whether about chariot ratios, grain requisition, or the discipline of flags and drums — and argues that a military reader is better served by less polished but more literal renderings.

Source · Ralph D. Sawyer, The Art of War (Westview, 1994), translator's notes; also his Seven Military Classics.
Rebuttal

Samuel Griffith, himself a U.S. Marine brigadier general, produced a translation for Oxford in 1963 that split the difference: closer to the Chinese than Giles, but still literary, and it has been widely used at war colleges without the complaints Sawyer levels.

Source · Samuel B. Griffith, Sun Tzu: The Art of War (Oxford University Press, 1963).
School ThreeIII

Ethical & Moral Complaints ▶

Deception as a cardinal virtue, spies as the "most important element," victory without fighting as a euphemism for coercion. Readers from Christian ethicists to feminist theorists have wondered whether the book's calm tone hides something colder.

Click to expand all critics in this school

Bruce Fleming

Professor of English, U.S. Naval Academy
Military Mysticism ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The reverence The Art of War inspires inside the U.S. military is a form of intellectual laziness — officers reach for Sun Tzŭ the way a freshman reaches for a fortune cookie, in place of doing the harder ethical reasoning their actual wars demand.

Argument

In his book Bridging the Military-Civilian Divide and in essays for The Chronicle of Higher Education, Fleming argues that service academies treat a handful of classics — Sun Tzŭ, Thucydides, Clausewitz — as decorative wisdom rather than as arguments to be tested, and that this produces officers who can quote the book but cannot morally interrogate it.

Source · Bruce Fleming, Bridging the Military-Civilian Divide (Potomac, 2010); and essays in The Chronicle of Higher Education.
Rebuttal

Defenders of the Naval War College curriculum point out that Sun Tzŭ is taught alongside just-war theory, international law, and Clausewitz — not in place of them — and that Fleming's complaint is really about bad teaching, not the book itself.

Source · Naval War College, Strategy and Policy syllabus materials (public releases).

Jessica Wolfendale

Professor of Philosophy, Case Western Reserve; military ethics
Ethics of Deception ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The book's elevation of deception (gui dao, 詭道, "the way of deception") as a foundational principle is in tension with modern just-war traditions that treat certain kinds of treachery as moral crimes, not clever virtues.

Argument

Writing on the ethics of military deception, Wolfendale argues that treating deception as an unqualified good — which she sees Sun Tzŭ's admirers as doing — collapses the crucial moral distinction between, say, camouflage and perfidy, and gives cover to practices the laws of armed conflict specifically forbid.

Source · Jessica Wolfendale, "Military Ethics," in the Routledge Handbook of Military Ethics; and related essays.
Rebuttal

Sun Tzŭ's defenders reply that his deception is overwhelmingly operational (feints, misdirection, concealed movement), not the treacherous abuse of flags of truce, and that the book's goal — "to subdue the enemy without fighting" — is actually more humane than rival traditions that glorify decisive battle.

Source · Michael I. Handel, Masters of War (Frank Cass, 2001), ch. on Sun Tzŭ and deception.

Laura Sjoberg

Professor of Political Science, University of Florida; feminist IR theorist
Feminist Reading ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Classical strategic texts — Sun Tzŭ included — treat "the general," "the ruler," and "the soldier" as unmarked male categories, and the work civilian women and non-combatants do to sustain war is simply invisible to their ledger of victory.

Argument

In her work on gender and the theory of war, Sjoberg argues that canonical strategic thought, from Sun Tzŭ onward, mistakes the view from the general's tent for the whole picture — erasing the gendered labor of supply, care, and aftermath that makes the campaign possible in the first place.

Source · Laura Sjoberg, Gender, Justice, and the Wars in Iraq (Lexington, 2006); and Gendering Global Conflict (Columbia UP, 2013).
Rebuttal

Sympathetic readers note the (perhaps apocryphal) anecdote in Sima Qian of Sun Wu drilling the king of Wu's concubines as soldiers — a story which, whatever its historicity, at least imagines women as capable of military discipline, unusual for its era.

Source · Sima Qian, Shiji, biography of Sun Wu, ch. 65.
School FourIV

The Pop-Applications Backlash ▶

Since the 1980s, Sun Tzŭ has been conscripted to sell tennis rackets, hostile takeovers, dating apps, and The Sopranos. A cottage industry of critics has formed around the sentence, "it's not actually about that."

Christopher Bassford

Professor of Strategy, U.S. National War College (retired); Clausewitz scholar
Anti-Guru ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The modern "Sun Tzŭ good, Clausewitz bad" slogan is a lazy trope of airport-bookshop strategy, based on reading neither author carefully.

Argument

On his long-running Clausewitz.com site and in Clausewitz in English, Bassford has written that the popular Eastern-vs-Western caricature — Sun Tzŭ the subtle sage, Clausewitz the bloody Prussian — serves neither man and usually means the writer has read a paragraph of each and Googled the rest.

Source · Christopher Bassford, Clausewitz in English (Oxford University Press, 1994); and essays at clausewitz.com.
Rebuttal

Michael Handel, before Bassford, argued that the two men are closer than either their fans or their detractors admit — which is itself a form of rescue for Sun Tzŭ's reputation against the guru industry Bassford mocks.

Source · Michael I. Handel, Masters of War: Classical Strategic Thought, 3rd ed. (Frank Cass, 2001).

Matthew Stewart

Philosopher; author of The Management Myth (W. W. Norton)
Business Gurus ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The management industry's love of Sun Tzŭ is part of a broader pattern in which business writers dress up banal advice in borrowed cultural prestige — and the costume of an ancient Chinese general happens to be especially cheap and flattering.

Argument

In The Management Myth, Stewart argues that management thinking has long laundered its pedestrian insights through philosophers, physicists, and warriors it does not understand, and that the "Sun Tzŭ for executives" shelf is a perfect specimen: old book, exotic setting, maxims abstract enough to mean whatever you want.

Source · Matthew Stewart, The Management Myth: Why the Experts Keep Getting it Wrong (W. W. Norton, 2009).
Rebuttal

Mark McNeilly, a former IBM executive and author of Sun Tzu and the Art of Business, has argued that the analogy between market competition and classical warfare is neither lazy nor dishonest — firms really do face intelligence problems, deception, terrain, and logistics, even if the stakes are measured in quarterly earnings rather than blood.

Source · Mark McNeilly, Sun Tzu and the Art of Business (Oxford University Press, 2011).

William Duggan

Senior Lecturer, Columbia Business School; author of Strategic Intuition
Strategy Fetish ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Business readers routinely miss what is actually strategic in Sun Tzŭ — the insistence on foreknowledge, patience, and refusing battles you don't need — and instead extract the parts that sound most like Glengarry Glen Ross.

Argument

Duggan has argued in his Columbia courses and in Strategic Intuition that managers who cite Sun Tzŭ tend to pick up the aggressive vocabulary ("attack," "enemy," "deceive") and drop the cautious frame — the book's insistence that the best victory is the one you did not need to purchase — producing a Sun Tzŭ who is, in effect, the opposite of Sun Tzŭ.

Source · William Duggan, Strategic Intuition: The Creative Spark in Human Achievement (Columbia Business School Publishing, 2007).
Rebuttal

Thomas Huynh of Sonshi.com and other popular interpreters concede the point and have spent twenty years trying to steer business readers back toward the restrained, intelligence-first Sun Tzŭ they think the book actually teaches.

Source · Thomas Huynh, The Art of War: Spirituality for Conflict (SkyLight Paths, 2008).
School FiveV

Comparative & Historical ▶

Historians who think Sun Tzŭ is — to put it bluntly — overrated: not useless, but not the universal strategic key its English-language cult makes it out to be. Some of these critics are themselves admirers of classical Chinese warfare; the complaint is about inflation, not contempt.

Michael I. Handel

Professor of National Security Studies, U.S. Naval War College; author of Masters of War
Not Universal ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Sun Tzŭ's precepts are genuinely insightful but much narrower than his cult admits: he assumes a single decisive campaign season, a unitary ruler, and a political culture in which battle is an extension of court politics — none of which map cleanly onto modern war.

Argument

In Masters of War, Handel treats Sun Tzŭ seriously enough to read him alongside Clausewitz and Jomini — and concludes that while the overlap between them is underestimated, the Sunzi has little to say about the things modern strategy cares about most: alliances, mobilization, industrial capacity, and the long political tail of war.

Source · Michael I. Handel, Masters of War: Classical Strategic Thought, 3rd ed. (Frank Cass, 2001).
Rebuttal

Sun Tzŭ's defenders note that Handel's own argument is that the two traditions are converging, not opposed; the book's silence on industrial war is hardly a defect in a two-and-a-half-millennia-old text, and its emphasis on intelligence and preparation scales unusually well.

Source · Handel, same book, concluding chapters.

Jeremy Black

Professor Emeritus of History, University of Exeter; prolific military historian
Eurocentrism Inverted ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

The West's enthusiasm for Sun Tzŭ is a kind of flattering Orientalism in reverse — a desire for an Eastern "deeper wisdom" that lets Western readers feel both cosmopolitan and tactical at once, while skipping the hard work of comparative military history.

Argument

In works such as War and the Cultural Turn, Black warns against universalizing any single classical text, Sun Tzŭ included, and argues that strategic cultures are locally specific and that treating a small Warring States manual as a master key distorts how war actually varies across place and period.

Source · Jeremy Black, War and the Cultural Turn (Polity, 2012); also Rethinking Military History (Routledge, 2004).
Rebuttal

Defenders reply that Black's own books quote Sun Tzŭ approvingly more than once, and that the point is not universality but unusual portability: few other ancient manuals have survived translation into so many strategic cultures while still producing useful arguments in each.

Source · Jeremy Black, A Short History of War (Yale University Press, 2021), passim.

Peter A. Lorge

Professor of Pre-Modern Chinese and Military History, Vanderbilt University
Historicize ▶
Click to read argument & rebuttal

Sun Tzŭ was, in traditional Chinese military education, one among seven canonical classics — not a solo star — and elevating him above the others is a Western distortion of the Chinese tradition itself.

Argument

In The Asian Military Revolution and his Chinese Martial Arts, Lorge argues that in the Wujing Qishu — the Seven Military Classics canonized under the Song — Sun Tzŭ shares shelf space with Wu Qi, Sima Rangju, the Wei Liaozi and others, and that the English-language fixation on Sun Tzŭ alone is a modern marketing accident, not a Chinese judgment.

Source · Peter A. Lorge, The Asian Military Revolution (Cambridge UP, 2008); and Chinese Martial Arts: From Antiquity to the Twenty-First Century (Cambridge UP, 2011).
Rebuttal

Ralph Sawyer, who translated the whole Seven Military Classics precisely to correct this imbalance, agrees with Lorge about the canon but also notes that Sun Tzŭ is routinely placed first in traditional Chinese lists for a reason — his chapters are the densest and the most general.

Source · Ralph D. Sawyer, The Seven Military Classics of Ancient China (Westview Press, 1993).
