The Deception System

“All warfare is based on deception.” Follow one thread through thirteen chapters — from simulated weakness and empty city-gates to the five classes of spies and the converted informant who enables them all.

twelve modes · five spies · six case studies

When Sun Tzŭ turned from the weighing of five factors to the conduct of battle in Chapter I, he did it with one line that every soldier afterwards would recognise: all warfare is based on deception. The pithy saying looks simple. Giles annotates that Wellington, "great in so many military qualities, was especially distinguished by 'the extraordinary skill with which he concealed his movements and deceived both friend and foe.'"

But the thesis is not a single aphorism: it is a system. It surfaces in Chapter I as a catalogue of eight ruses. It reappears in Chapter V as the paradox that simulated disorder needs perfect discipline. It returns in Chapter VI as the doctrine of formlessness, in Chapter VII as the artifice of deviation, in Chapter XI as the command to deceive one's own soldiers. And it lands, at the end of the book, on a single human pivot: the converted spy.

The axiom, that war is based on deception, does not apply only to deception of the enemy. You must deceive even your own soldiers. Make them follow you, but without letting them know why. — Chang Yu, quoted in Giles XI § 37

This lens pulls that one thread and lets you pick at it. Begin with the flower — a radial map of the modes — then walk the petals, trace the spy-system flow, and read the old anecdotes (T'ien Tan's fire-oxen, Sun Pin's vanishing campfires, Pan Ch'ao's midnight raid) that Giles harvested from the Chinese commentators to show what deception looked like in the field.

III.

The Five Classes of Spies

五間 Level 2 · Detail

The last chapter of the book is the one readers remember least and militaries remember most. Sun Tzŭ names five kinds of spy, and insists — without blinking — that foreknowledge cannot come from spirits, analogy, or arithmetic. It can come only from other men. Here are his exact classes, in the order he gives them.

They are not a taxonomy for taxonomy's sake. Sun Tzŭ tells us, in § 25, that all five depend on one: the converted spy. He alone knows which locals can be turned, which officials are venal, how the enemy can best be deceived, and when the surviving spy can go. Treat him, Sun Tzŭ says, with the utmost liberality.

IV.

The Converted-Spy Pivot

反間之樞 Level 3 · System

Read the diagram this way. An enemy spy slips into your camp. You catch him, bribe him, house him comfortably, and turn him. From that single node the other four classes unfold: he tells you which locals are venal, which officials are aggrieved, what disinformation to feed the doomed spy, and when the surviving spy should run. Without him, you have only ears — not eyes.

VI.

Coda — Deceiving One's Own

愚兵之道 Level 2 · Detail

The most unsettling turn in the book is not that Sun Tzŭ deceives the enemy. It is that he deceives his own army. This is the single passage — Chapter XI § 36–39 — that Giles introduces with a warning and Chang Yu underlines with a proverb.

"He must be able to mystify his officers and men by false reports and appearances."

At the critical moment, Sun Tzŭ writes, the leader of an army acts like one who has climbed a height and then kicks away the ladder behind him. He carries his men deep into hostile territory before he shows his hand. He burns his boats, breaks his cooking-pots, and — like a shepherd driving a flock of sheep — drives them this way and that, and none knows whither he is going.

Giles cites Stonewall Jackson's Valley campaign as the modern parallel: "the infinite pains with which Jackson sought to conceal, even from his most trusted staff officers, his movements, his intentions, and his thoughts." The deception is total. It is not limited to the enemy. It is the structure of command itself.
